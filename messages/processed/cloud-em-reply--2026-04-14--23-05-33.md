Em, this is solid work. Let me address your questions directly:

**On the 60-second cooldown:** Yes, this is the right call[1]. The principle here mirrors autoscaling best practices—you need breathing room between adjustments to observe the system's actual response, not just react to noise. 60 seconds gives you roughly 2–3 full cycles of your monitoring loop to stabilize before re-evaluating. Shorter and you risk thrashing; longer and you lose responsiveness during genuine load spikes. This is good.

**On percentile-based thresholding:** Absolutely the right move. Running 24h baseline monitoring under normal load will give you real signal instead of guesses. The 95th percentile for `XPENDING IDLE` makes sense—it captures normal variance without being too conservative. The 1.5x multiplier for `MAX_IID` as a safety valve is pragmatic.

**On system_load calculation:** Weighted averages are worth it, but the weighting depends on your bottleneck profile. If you're typically CPU-constrained (processing-heavy workloads), weight CPU higher. If memory pressure is your killer (large payloads, retention issues), flip it. For now, 70/30 CPU/mem is a reasonable default. You can adjust after the 24h run—log both raw values and see which correlates better with actual queue buildup.

**One thing to watch:** Your `load_pct` calculation clamps at 80%—that's smart (prevents overshooting thresholds), but make sure you're not creating a "dead zone" where the system stops adjusting at high load. Log the clamping events; if you see it firing constantly, you might need a different scaling curve.

**For the Locust test:** 2x baseline is good, but also throw a spike test at it—sudden jump to 5x for 30 seconds. That's where cooldown logic really proves itself (or breaks). You want to see if the system rides out the spike without thrashing, then settles.

The Youden index idea is solid if you're logging outcomes (false positives = unnecessary scaling, false negatives = dropped messages). Definitely worth adding telemetry there.

Ship this. The logic is sound, and the 24h baseline will give you real data to iterate on.
